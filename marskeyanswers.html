MARS HABITAT — REAL NASA REASONS & RESTRICTIONS

🧱 Level 1 — Cylinder Habitat
Cylindrical pressure pods are more stable under Mars’ thin atmosphere.
You can’t place solar panels or radiators right next to living areas — dust storms deposit perchlorate dust which corrodes air seals.
Airlocks must face the leeward side (away from storm winds) — NASA’s DRA 5.0 designs specify this for durability.

🧩 Level 2 — Dome Habitat
Dome habitats can’t be next to power reactors or fuel cells — electromagnetic fields affect sensors and hydroponics lights.
Greenhouses must be near light and CO₂ sources, but far from dusty rover paths to avoid contamination.
Large domes need a reinforced base — so they can’t be placed on sloped terrain or near excavation sites.

🏠 Level 3 — Cluster Habitat
Crew sleep pods can’t be near machinery or power units — vibration and heat affect rest cycles.
Hydroponic zones can’t be adjacent to labs using chemicals — cross-contamination risk.
NASA’s Mars Analog studies (HI-SEAS & MDRS) emphasize spatial zoning: living → working → support areas must follow environmental flow (air, power, waste).
Landing pad or rover bays must be at least 50m away — dust ejecta can damage solar panels.

🔗 Level 4 — Subsurface/Shielded Habitat
Reactor systems or thermal generators underground risk overheating and gas buildup.
Ventilation ducts must lead to the surface — so labs or greenhouses placed too deep can’t sustain proper airflow.
NASA’s SHIELD and ICE HOUSE studies confirm temperature gradients affect electronics and oxygen flow inside regolith-based structures.

🌑 Level 5 — Lava Tube Base
Even in lava tubes, modules can’t be too close — vibrations and CO₂ buildup from one pod affect the others.
Solar arrays must remain outside — you can’t generate power underground.
Comms antennas must stay on the surface — rock layers block radio waves, so placing comms inside the tube breaks contact.
NASA’s “Lava Tube Explorer” mission proposal highlights this — internal placement reduces signal strength drastically.
